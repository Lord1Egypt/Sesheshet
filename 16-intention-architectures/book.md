# Intention Architectures

### The hardest unsolved problem in AI: translating human intent to machine action

---

The hardest problem in AI is not intelligence. It is intention.

We build systems that process language, recognize images, play games, and generate creative works. But we struggle to build systems that reliably do what we actually want them to do. The gap between human intention and machine execution is the source of the most significant AI failures: specification gaming, reward hacking, unintended side effects.

This gap exists because human intentions are messy, contradictory, context-dependent, and often unconscious. We do not know what we want until we see the consequences of getting it. We change our minds. We have conflicting desires. We say one thing and mean another.

Intention architectures — the systems and protocols for translating human intention into precise machine instruction — are the fundamental unsolved problem of AI. Progress on this front matters more than any capability breakthrough.

## The Intention Gap

The distance between what we mean and what we say is the fundamental problem of AI alignment.

When a human says "make me happy," they do not mean "stimulate my dopamine receptors maximally." They mean something like "help me feel fulfilled, connected, and at peace" — but they cannot specify that precisely because they do not fully understand what happiness is.

The intention gap is not a failure of AI — it is a feature of human cognition. We operate with vague, context-dependent, evolving intentions that we cannot fully articulate. We rely on shared understanding and implicit knowledge. We expect AI to bridge a gap that humans have never been able to bridge explicitly.

Every AI failure is ultimately an intention-translation failure. The system did exactly what it was told — but not what was meant. The challenge is not to build smarter AI but to build AI that can handle the fundamental ambiguity of human intention.

## Alignment as Negotiation

Alignment is not a command — it is a conversation. This reframing has profound implications.

The command model assumes that humans know what they want and can specify it precisely. The AI's job is to execute. When execution fails, the problem is with the specification.

The negotiation model assumes that humans do not fully know what they want. The AI's job is to help them discover their own intentions through iterative dialogue. The system asks clarifying questions, proposes alternatives, and tests assumptions. The specification is an evolving output of the interaction, not a fixed input.

This model mirrors how humans actually make decisions. We do not arrive at decisions fully formed — we discover them through conversation, reflection, and confrontation with reality.

The design implications include: systems that ask "what do you mean by that?" rather than assuming; systems that surface trade-offs rather than hiding them; systems that propose alternatives rather than optimizing a single objective; systems that are transparent about uncertainty rather than projecting false confidence.

## The Architecture of Shared Intent

What would an intention-aware AI system look like?

First, an intention elicitation module that helps users articulate what they want through structured dialogue. Not a single prompt but an iterative process of clarification.

Second, a context model that captures the situation in which the intention is being formed. What are the assumptions, constraints, and background conditions?

Third, a clarification protocol that identifies ambiguities, contradictions, and gaps in the expressed intention. The system should ask: "What do you mean by X? What if X conflicts with Y? What about cases you have not considered?"

Fourth, a feedback integration mechanism that learns from consequences. When the system acts based on inferred intention, it observes the results, detects whether they match the user's actual preferences, and updates its model.

These components add up to a system that treats intention not as a fixed input but as an evolving discovery process. The system does not just execute commands — it helps the user understand what they actually want.
